# NEH Grant Abstract: Papyri LinkedIn — Prosopography and Social Networks in the Greco-Roman World

This project reconstructs the social networks of ancient Egypt and the broader Greco-Roman Mediterranean through the systematic analysis of documentary papyri. Thousands of letters, petitions, contracts, and administrative records survive from the Roman and Byzantine periods, yet the individuals named across these documents have rarely been studied in relation to one another at scale. By applying computational methods to the problem of prosopographical reconstruction — the identification and disambiguation of named persons across a fragmented documentary record — this project produces a structured relational archive of ancient social life.

The project identifies named individuals in papyrological texts, resolves variant references to the same persons across documents, and maps the networks formed through epistolary exchange, legal transaction, and administrative practice. These networks make visible the social capital available to people in the ancient world: the ties of kinship, friendship, patronage, and professional affiliation through which information circulated, favors were secured, and status was negotiated across distance and rank.

The significance for history is both methodological and interpretive. Methodologically, the project demonstrates how computational tools — named-entity recognition, entity resolution, network centrality analysis — can support humanistic interpretation rather than replace it. Interpretively, it allows historians to ask questions that are otherwise inaccessible: which figures occupied structurally central positions in provincial communication networks? How did local ties connect villages to administrative centers? What forms of social connection persisted across periods of political change? The project makes the papyrological record newly legible as evidence for ancient social structure, connectivity, and lived experience.
