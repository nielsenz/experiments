# Papyri LinkedIn: Prosopographical Reconstruction and Epistolary Networks in the Greco-Roman World

The documentary papyri of Roman and Byzantine Egypt preserve an extraordinary archive of ancient social life. Letters, petitions, tax receipts, contracts, and administrative memoranda record not only institutions and transactions but also the people who moved through them: kin, neighbors, clients, officials, brokers, scribes, and correspondents. Yet this evidence is dispersed across thousands of fragmentary documents, each of which captures only a momentary trace of a broader human world. The Papyri LinkedIn project addresses this problem by combining close reading with computational methods to reconstruct the relational structure of ancient society at a scale that is impossible through manual scholarship alone.

At its core, the project is a prosopographical undertaking. It identifies named individuals in papyrological texts, resolves variant references to the same person across disparate records, and organizes those identifications into a scholarly dataset that can be read critically and revised transparently. This is not simply a technical exercise in named-entity recognition or entity resolution. It is a historically grounded attempt to recover persons from the archive: to determine when a “Maria” in one petition may be the same woman as a “Maria daughter of Theon” in another, or when repeated references to an official, scribe, or landholder reveal a durable role within a local social world. In this sense, the project advances the long-standing humanistic goal of prosopographical reconstruction while extending it to a documentary corpus too large and uneven for exhaustive manual comparison.

The interpretive value of the project lies in the networks it makes visible. Papyrological texts are often read as isolated witnesses to administration, family life, or economic exchange. By linking persons across documents, the project maps epistolary networks, administrative ties, and patterns of association that show how people actually navigated the Greco-Roman world. These networks reveal social capital in its ancient form: who could rely on whom, how requests traveled, where influence clustered, and which individuals served as connectors between villages, towns, and bureaucratic centers. Such questions matter for history because they illuminate the lived mechanisms of empire, the social infrastructure of communication, and the uneven distribution of access, authority, and mobility.

The project therefore moves beyond the claim that digital humanities can “visualize” the past. A graph, by itself, is not the scholarly outcome. The scholarly contribution is a source-critical method for using computational analysis to support interpretation. Network centrality, for example, is not treated as a self-explanatory measure of importance. It is a heuristic that helps historians identify figures whose documentary presence suggests unusual relational reach, institutional mediation, or brokerage, and then return to the texts to ask why those patterns emerged. Likewise, computational entity resolution does not replace philological judgment; it creates a documented framework within which identifications can be evaluated, contested, and refined. The result is an interpretive infrastructure that makes the evidence more legible without flattening its complexity.

For NEH purposes, the project is significant because it answers a historical question of broad consequence: how did social relations structure life in the ancient Mediterranean, and how can we recover those relations from a fragmentary archive? The papyri preserve evidence for everyday negotiations of power, trust, rank, and dependency. By assembling that evidence into a relational corpus, the project allows scholars to study patterns that are otherwise invisible: the overlap of kinship and office, the persistence of local intermediaries, the circulation of requests and favors, and the social reach of individuals who otherwise appear only briefly in the record. These insights matter not only for papyrologists and classicists, but for historians of the ancient world more generally, because they deepen our understanding of how imperial systems were experienced at the level of ordinary interaction.

Papyri LinkedIn thus contributes a reusable scholarly resource and a model for humanities computing that is accountable to interpretation. It is designed to support future research, teaching, and comparative work across ancient history, documentary studies, and digital humanities. Most importantly, it shows that computational methods can do more than generate visualizations: they can expand the historical imagination by helping scholars reconstruct the social worlds that ancient texts only partially preserve.
